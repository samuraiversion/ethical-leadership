About This Research

My name is Yutong Chen, and I am a postgraduate student in the School of Management at the University of Bristol. This research forms part of my MSc dissertation, supervised by Dr Karina Pavlisa.

This study is concerned with a question that most working professionals will recognise: how do we come to judge whether the person leading us is genuinely ethical? Rather than examining leaders in isolation, I am interested in the follower's perspective — the specific behaviours, moments, and contextual cues that lead people to trust, question, or respect a supervisor's integrity and fairness in practice.

The research draws on established scholarship in ethical leadership and adopts an inductive, qualitative approach. Rather than testing a fixed set of hypotheses, I want to understand your experiences in your own terms — and allow patterns and insights to emerge from genuine, open conversation.

The Three Research Questions Guiding This Study
RQ 1
What behaviours and actions do followers identify as signals of ethical leadership in their professional settings?
RQ 2
How do followers construct, interpret, and revise these perceptions of ethical leadership over the course of supervisory relationships?
RQ 3
What individual and contextual factors shape the way followers construct their perceptions of ethical leadership, and with what consequences for their own sense of professional responsibility?

What Participation Involves

If you agree to take part, here is what will happen:

📩 Before You receive this information sheet and a consent form. We agree a convenient date and time for your interview.
🎙 During A 30–45 minute online conversation about your professional experiences. You will be asked to reflect on supervisors you have worked with. No preparation is needed.
📄 After The recording will be transcribed and anonymised. You may request to review your transcript and withdraw any statements before analysis begins.

The interview will be audio-recorded with your permission. You may ask for the recording to be paused or stopped at any point. No real names of people or organisations need to be mentioned during the interview — you may refer to anyone as "my manager" or "a previous employer."

Why This Research Matters — and What You May Gain

Ethical leadership is not simply an academic concept. It shapes whether people feel safe speaking up at work, whether they trust their organisation's decisions, and whether they feel their contributions are genuinely valued. By understanding how followers actually perceive and interpret ethical leadership — rather than relying solely on surveys and abstract measurements — this research aims to produce insights that are grounded in real professional experience.

Workplace Culture
The findings may help organisations understand what leadership behaviours actually build trust and psychological safety — going beyond generic frameworks to reflect what followers genuinely respond to.
Leadership Development
By identifying the specific signals followers use to assess ethical leadership, this research can inform more targeted and meaningful training programmes for managers at all levels.
Employee Experience
Understanding how ethical leadership is perceived — and what happens when it falls short — can support efforts to improve employee wellbeing, reduce disengagement, and create fairer working environments.
Academic Contribution
This study addresses a recognised gap in the literature: most existing research measures leaders' behaviour, but rarely explores how followers construct these perceptions themselves. Your experience contributes directly to filling that gap.

What you may personally gain from taking part: Leadership — particularly the question of what makes it genuinely ethical — is something most people have strong feelings about, yet rarely get the chance to discuss in a structured way. This interview offers that space. You may find it useful to reflect on experiences that have shaped how you work, what you value in a workplace, or how you think about your own responsibilities as a professional. There are no right or wrong answers, and no particular outcome is expected from your participation — but we hope the conversation itself feels worthwhile.

Potential risks: There are no significant risks associated with participation. The interview touches on professional experiences, which may occasionally bring to mind more challenging workplace situations. You are under no obligation to discuss anything you find uncomfortable, and you may stop or pause the interview at any time without explanation.
